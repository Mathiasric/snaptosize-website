## FAQ Topics (8 questions)

1. **Can I resize prints for Etsy in Canva for free?** — Yes, Canva Free lets you create custom-sized canvases, but you must manually create and export each size individually. There is no batch export for multiple print sizes.

2. **How many sizes should I include in an Etsy printable listing?** — Top sellers include sizes across multiple ratios (2:3, 3:4, 4:5, ISO, and common extras). More sizes means more buyers can use your art with their frames. Link to /how-many-sizes-etsy-printable.

3. **Does Canva export at 300 DPI?** — Canva Pro allows PDF export at 300 DPI. Free accounts are limited to lower-quality exports. For JPG/PNG, Canva does not guarantee 300 DPI at specific print dimensions — you need to manually calculate pixel dimensions.

4. **What is the fastest way to resize one artwork into multiple print sizes?** — Specialized production tools can generate all standard print sizes from a single upload in under 30 seconds, compared to 20-30 minutes of manual resizing per artwork.

5. **Do I need Photoshop to resize images for Etsy?** — No. There are several alternatives ranging from free tools like Canva and GIMP to specialized Etsy production tools. The key difference is manual (one at a time) vs automated (all sizes at once).

6. **Why do Etsy printables need multiple aspect ratios?** — Standard frames come in different ratios. An 8x10 is 4:5, a 4x6 is 2:3, and an A4 is ISO. If you only offer one ratio, buyers with different frames cannot use your art. Link to /etsy-print-ratios.

7. **Can I use Canva to create art and SnapToSize to resize it?** — Yes. Many sellers design in Canva, export their highest-resolution master file, then use SnapToSize to produce all print sizes at once. The two tools are complementary.

8. **What file format should Etsy printables be in?** — JPG is the most common for printable wall art — it keeps files under Etsy's 20MB limit while maintaining print quality at 300 DPI. Link to /best-file-format-etsy-printables.

## Differentiation Angle

**This is NOT a generic "Tool A vs Tool B" feature comparison.** The angle is: Canva is a design tool, SnapToSize is a production tool. They solve different problems in the same workflow. Most comparison articles pit tools against each other — this page frames them as complementary stages in an Etsy seller's workflow (create in Canva, produce in SnapToSize). This reframes the buying decision from "should I switch?" to "should I add this to my workflow?" — a much lower friction conversion.

The page is also educational-first: the buyer expectations section (what sizes, what ratios, what file quality) provides standalone value even if the reader never converts. This supports SEO authority and reduces bounce rate.
